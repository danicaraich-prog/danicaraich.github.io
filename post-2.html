Nuclear Diplomacy

What several former U.S. diplomats had to say about the current U.S. strategy on nuclear weapons

Posted by Danica Raich on August 24, 2025

As an intern with the American Academy of Diplomacy, I had the opportunity to interview several leading figures in U.S. national security and diplomacy: Ambassador Bonnie Jenkins, Ambassador Rose Gottemoeller, Ambassador Thomas Countryman, and Admiral James Foggo.

Our conversations ranged from the basics of chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear (CBRN) weapons to naval strategy, treaty negotiation, and the future of international arms control. Speaking with practitioners who have shaped U.S. policy at the highest levels offered a rare window into how diplomacy and national security policy actually function in practice.

Below are a few highlights from each conversation.

Ambassador Rose Gottemoeller

Amb. Rose Gottemoeller served as NATO’s Deputy Secretary General and previously as the U.S. Under Secretary of State for Arms Control and International Security, where she was the chief U.S. negotiator for the New START Treaty with Russia.

Our conversation focused on how nuclear policy has evolved since the dawn of the nuclear age and what challenges lie ahead for arms control and strategic stability.

Amb. Gottemoeller began by reflecting on what she described as the “nuclear revolution.” When the first atomic bomb was detonated nearly eighty years ago, it introduced a level of destructive capability previously unimaginable. For the first time, a single weapon could produce devastation on a scale that once required massive conventional bombing campaigns. Despite this extraordinary power, nuclear deterrence has remained relatively stable for decades. This stability is reinforced not only by strategic balance but also by what many scholars call the “nuclear taboo," which is a term used to describe the powerful global norm against the use of nuclear weapons.

At the same time, nuclear deterrence has never prevented all conflict. Amb. Gottemoeller emphasized that conventional wars continue even in a nuclear-armed world, pointing to the ongoing war in Ukraine as a reminder that geopolitical competition persists despite nuclear deterrence.

Another key theme of our discussion was the importance of securing fissile material. Preventing nuclear terrorism depends heavily on ensuring that the materials required to build nuclear weapons remain tightly controlled. Amb. Gottemoeller noted that reports of attempted nuclear material smuggling in places like Georgia demonstrate that this threat remains real and ongoing.

We also discussed modernization of the U.S. nuclear triad—intercontinental ballistic missiles, strategic bombers, and nuclear submarines. Because many of these systems were developed decades ago, modernization is necessary to maintain a safe, secure, and effective deterrent. At the same time, she emphasized that modernization should continue within the framework of arms control agreements such as the New START Treaty, which places limits on deployed nuclear warheads and delivery systems for both the United States and Russia.

Looking ahead, Amb. Gottemoeller highlighted the growing role of China in the nuclear landscape. China’s arsenal is expanding and could reach roughly 1,500 warheads by 2035. However, she cautioned against alarmism. The United States retains significant advantages, and the priority should be pragmatic engagement rather than panic. Recent discussions among the five recognized nuclear-weapon states suggest there may be opportunities to expand dialogue on military doctrine and risk reduction.

Ultimately, Amb. Gottemoeller emphasized that future arms control efforts should build on existing frameworks while exploring new confidence-building measures. Tools such as launch notification agreements, crisis hotlines, and greater transparency could help reduce the risk of miscalculation between nuclear powers and strengthen long-term strategic stability.

Ambassador Bonnie Jenkins

Ambassador Bonnie Jenkins served as the U.S. Under Secretary of State for Arms Control and International Security and previously founded Women of Color Advancing Peace and Security (WCAPS). Our conversation focused on the global architecture for preventing the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction.

Amb. Jenkins explained how international institutions and treaties work together to prevent the spread of CBRN weapons, emphasizing the importance of cooperation between governments, scientists, and international organizations. She also highlighted the growing importance of inclusive leadership in security policy, arguing that diverse perspectives strengthen international problem-solving and diplomacy.

Thomas Countryman

Thomas Countryman served as the U.S. Assistant Secretary of State for International Security and Nonproliferation and currently chairs the Arms Control Association.

In our discussion, Mr. Countryman provided a clear overview of nuclear nonproliferation and the role of international agreements such as the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT). He emphasized the importance of maintaining global norms against the spread of nuclear weapons and explained how diplomacy and multilateral cooperation work together to uphold those norms.

Admiral James Foggo

Adm. Foggo is a retired four-star admiral in the U.S. Navy who previously commanded U.S. Naval Forces Europe and Africa as well as Allied Joint Force Command Naples.

Our conversation focused on the strategic role of naval forces in maintaining global security and deterring conflict. Adm. Foggo discussed how maritime strategy intersects with diplomacy, alliance management, and international law, particularly in contested regions such as the Mediterranean and the Black Sea. He also highlighted the importance of NATO cooperation and the role of naval power in supporting broader national security objectives.
