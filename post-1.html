Liberation from the Cave

The Role of Education in Democracy 

Written by Danica Raich for the 2023 ACTC Conference in Boston, MA

In Plato’s Republic, Socrates tells Glaucon to “make an image of our nature in its education and want of education, likening it to the condition of the following kind. See human beings as though they were in an underground cave […]” (514a). Prisoners are bound since birth, unable to turn their heads in any direction, and forced to face the cave’s wall where they view shadows projected there from behind by an assortment of “puppet-handlers” (514a). Socrates concludes that “such men would hold that the truth is nothing other than the shadows of artificial things” (515c). 

Glaucon finds this image to be very strange, but Socrates defends that these prisoners are just like us. Like the prisoners in Socrates’ cave, we are confused about our own existence and are confined to the expectations of the world around us, just as the prisoners are limited to what the puppet-handlers show on the walls of the cave. When one of the prisoners breaks out of the cave, they are forced to recognize the implications of their own reality. At first, the prisoner is reluctant to progress out of the confines of the cave and into the sunlight and to conceive of the truth of the world. When prisoners free themselves from the cave, release requires metastrophe— a “turning around” of the soul (518d). 

Ascent from the cave into the light is painful but liberates one from the world of shadows and rhetoric into one of being and truth—though it is a struggle to see the truth and to be liberated by the chains that bind us to the comforts of our perception of reality. This philosophical enlightenment or the emergence from the cave can be likened to the idea of noesis as introduced in the allegory of the divided line. According to Plato it is by dialectic that one can rise to “the end of the journey”— to noesis (532e). 

Dialectic or education, then, is a way in which to “illuminate the truth” (508d). It gives one the power to know “the idea of the good”— to perceive both knowledge and truth of the intelligible realm. Plato’s allegory of the cave is the inception of the idea of the liberating power of education—as the cave represents states that arise in the human soul– noesis, dianoia, pistis and eikasia. Thus, the pursuit of knowledge through core texts is an ascent to liberation, just as ascent from the cave is an ascent of the soul. Plato asserts that the aim of education is precisely what we gain from reading classic texts, as education “indicates that this power is in the soul of each and that the instrument with which each learns—just as an eye is not able to turn toward the light from the dark without the whole body— must be turned around from that which is coming into being together with the whole soul until it is able to endure looking at that which is and the brightest part of that which is” (518c). 

This is congruent to the life of the philosopher engaged with core texts, to be one to escape physical perception of the world is to understand what it means to be a human being influenced by the constructs of society and rhetoric. To achieve a reflective understanding of the world, we must break from convention and personal convictions, which starts with the reading of classic texts that engage us in a millennia long conversation about questions of human existence and the nature of our own reality. 

Although it was written over 2,000 years ago, The Republic’s sentiments on education, justice, and liberation still resonate with us today. When Athens was in a time of government corruption, instability, and uncertainty, Plato reimagines Socrates as he foresaw Athens succumbing to tyranny and authoritarian rule from its democratic state. By challenging dominant narratives on justice, the nature of the polis, and the human soul, Plato engages in a conversation that has spanned across lifetimes—granting us an invitation to enter into a dialogue that has continued to the present day.

Alexis de Tocqueville follows Plato’s ideas on the pitfalls of democracy—demonstrating that education is necessary to prevent dissent into tyrannical rule. Much like Plato, Tocqueville fears tyranny of the majority from democracy, stating that he knows no country in which there is “less independence of mind and true freedom of discussion than in America” (257).

Tocqueville offers the perspective that in America there is a greater need for education through core texts due to the very nature of democracy—in which individualism, despotism, and tyranny of the majority prevail—and provides a way to combat the American way of automatically deferring to the opinion of the majority in Chapter XV: "The Study of Greek And Latin Literature Is Peculiarly Useful In Democratic Communities.” He presents a solution to the individualism and vindication of self-interest of the American mind, in which the greatest intellectual authority is our own reason and self-esteem requires that we are not at odds with the majority. One of the greatest counterforces to the lack of intellectual independence Tocqueville points to lies in a liberal education. 

Alexis de Tocqueville states in Volume II of Democracy in America: 

  “And indeed a very superficial survey of the literary remains of the ancients will suffice to convince us, that if those writers were sometimes deficient in variety, or fertility in their subjects, or in boldness, vivacity, or power of generalization in their thoughts, they always displayed exquisite care and skill in their details. Nothing in their works seems to be done hastily or at random: every line is written for the eye of the connoisseur, and is shaped after some conception of ideal beauty. No literature places those fine qualities, in which the writers of democracies are naturally deficient, in bolder relief than that of the ancients; no literature, therefore, ought to be more studied in democratic ages” (I.II.542).

  To counter the defects of democratic society, in which education is driven by only its practical applications and utility, Tocqueville prescribes intellectual fulfillment through reading the classics, stating that, “All who aspire to literary excellence in democratic nations, ought frequently to refresh themselves at the springs of ancient literature: there is no more wholesome course for the mind” (I.II.543). To read Plato’s Republic or Aristotle’s Nichomachean Ethics is to understand a perspective on what it means live a good life and fundamental principles about what makes us human, to meditate on the virtues and function of humans and their intersection with happiness. 

Engaging in contemplation about the constraints of our society that the core texts highlight allows us to become liberated from the layers of conventions that shape our reality, whether those conventions are guided by the political, economic, or philosophical spheres of the world. As the authors involved in the great conversation of human existence insist, education is about self-knowledge and the nature of the good. These texts ask: How do we know what we say we know? What does it mean to be human? What exactly is the good life? What is the nature of the state? What is beauty? What is real? In search of the answers to such questions, an individual must make a voluntary decision to partake in the dialogue with classic texts, which is the true meaning of what it means to be liberated by education. 

Plato’s Republic and Tocqueville’s Democracy in America examine the necessity of a liberal education and its ability to allow us to gain a deeper understanding of the nature of reality and knowledge and offer perspective as to what educational liberation can do for those who are brave enough to begin the journey to sunlight from the comforts of the cave. Liberal education is more than simply academic intellectualism but encapsulates a conversation of shared vocabulary and human questions—showing the interplay between differing life experiences and cultural frameworks of interpretation. The transformative power of liberal education works in the same manner as a prisoner being freed from Socrates’ cave. Education is dependent on an individual’s willingness to pursue self-knowledge. 

Classical texts transcend the boundaries of time, place, and circumstance—and through them our preconceived notions about life are challenged, developed, and transformed. However, The Republic and Democracy in America operate in tandem with a conversation about humanity’s greatest questions of existence, as no one author is able to perfectly articulate the solutions to these challenges. While these books do not solve our problems for us, they provide insight into our most significant difficulties, where no proposition is left unexamined. These propositions have been explored and scrutinized in core texts, where authors have interacted with one another throughout the course of history. 

In his book, The Great Conversation: The substance of a liberal education, Robert Maynard Hutchins describes the core texts involved in the conversation as ones that “had endured and that the common voice of mankind called the finest creations, in writing, of the Western mind” (xi). He also explains that “from epoch to epoch, new books have been written that have won their place in the list […] It is the task of every generation to reassess the tradition in which it lives, to discard what it cannot use, and to bring into context with the distant and intermediate past the most recent contributions to the Great Conversation” (xi). Great thinkers exercise great thought in literature, which are bound by community of new branches of knowledge and vocabulary and the spirit of inquiry. 

Joining the Great Conversation

As voices in this Great Conversation, Plato and Tocqueville deal with the issues that humanity continues to grapple with today. By engaging with a liberal education, the student enters the conversation with the great writers, poets, and thinkers of human history—gaining a greater understanding of what it means to be human. Regardless of the time and culture, Plato and Tocqueville can be placed in conversation with each other—as they both warn that without the assistance of liberal education, we could fall into a misguided, anti-intellectual society that would prevent human flourishing. Thus, reading and conversing about classic texts allows one to liberate themselves from ignorance, equipping them with the knowledge of literature, philosophy, history, and the natural sciences and a deeper comprehension of the world around them.
