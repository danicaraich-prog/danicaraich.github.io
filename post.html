Breaking Free from the Military-Industrial Complex’s Nuclear Grip

Wirtten by Danica Raich on December 11, 2024 as a submission for the Pittsburgh Post Gazette

In 1961, President Dwight D. Eisenhower warned Americans of the dangers posed by the military-industrial complex—a powerful coalition of defense contractors, military leaders, and policymakers capable of influencing national priorities for profit rather than public good. His caution was clear: unchecked, this system can bend democracy and drive an endless cycle of militarization. More than sixty years later, former President Donald Trump’s
unorthodox presidency has thrust this long-simmering issue back into the spotlight.

In his previous term, Trump railed against “ridiculous endless wars” and criticized defense contractors for overspending. Yet under his leadership, defense budgets swelled, and the $2 trillion multiyear Sentinel Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM) program —a cornerstone of America’s nuclear modernization efforts—advanced substantially.

In 2025, Trump will once again face this trillion-dollar dilemma that affects not only how taxpayer dollars are spent but also how America chooses to engage with the world.

Undue Influence

The Sentinel ICBM program has already had a mix of delays and overruns, including
an 81% increase in the cost of the project. The program’s budget ballooned from $96 billion to $130 billion, and even triggered a review under the Nunn–McCurdy Act—a mechanism meant to restrain defense overspending, which should have terminated the program altogether. Yet Congress has so far avoided holding hearings or seriously investigating the cost overruns— reflecting a broader bipartisan reluctance to scrutinize the exploding costs of nuclear modernization, now projected to total $756 billion over the next decade.

Supporters of the program can be found within the influence of the Heritage
Foundation, who claim it is essential to maintain strategic parity with nuclear rivals like China and Russia. However, critics of the program make a compelling case—questioning whether land-based ICBMs like Sentinel are even necessary. They point to the U.S.'s current overwhelming nuclear capability and the dangers of the military-industrial complex driving
policy for profit.

Daniel Ellsberg, author of The Doomsday Machine and known to many as the original whistleblower, said in an interview with the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists:

“You would not have these arsenals, in the U.S. or elsewhere, if it were not the case that it was highly profitable to the military-industrial complex, to the aerospace industry, to the electronics industry, and to the weapons design labs to keep modernizing these weapons, improving accuracy, improving launch time, all that.”

The financial incentives for contractors are significant. General Dynamics, Honeywell, Lockheed Martin, and Northrop Grumman will do everything in their power to keep their large chunk of profit. Since 2018, ICBM contractors have spent over $226 million on lobbying efforts, employing hundreds of lobbyists to secure congressional backing for Sentinel.

Furthermore, this profit-driven approach to nuclear modernization stands in sharp contrast to what experts believe is actually needed for deterrence. Nuclear deterrence is not a simple numbers game, as the Biden Administration’s National Security Adviser Jake Sullivan points out, the U.S. does not need to increase nuclear forces nor “deploy
ever-more dangerous” weapons to maintain deterrence.

A Surprising Call for Disarmament

This debate isn’t just about dollars and weapons; it’s about who shapes America’s priorities and how the nation engages with the world. Eisenhower’s fears have materialized in programs like Sentinel, but his vision for a more balanced approach to defense remains within reach. Programs like Sentinel highlight the risks of allowing corporate interests to dominate decisions about national security. Left unchecked, this influence could
perpetuate unnecessary spending and lock the U.S. into an unrelenting arms race.

Ironically, former President Trump might be the best thing to happen to the campaign for nuclear disarmament. His willingness to defy conventional norms and challenge entrenched systems uniquely positions him to confront the military-industrial complex. The former President Trump recently expressed a need for global nuclear disarmament in a recent interview, “It only takes one. They have five countries now that have capability, and soon they’ll have more. And we shouldn’t let there be more.”

A Trump-led effort to prioritize diplomacy over stockpile expansion would mark a significant departure from his previous term and should serve as a foundation for bipartisan action.

Re-engaging with Russia on a successor to New START would be critical for preventing a new arms race, especially as nuclear-armed states continue to modernize their arsenals.
At the same time, initiating dialogue with China, which has historically avoided arms control negotiations, would mark a necessary step in addressing the growing trilemma of nuclear competition. These efforts wouldn’t just mitigate the risk of conflict; they would also undercut the rationale for costly programs like Sentinel, which thrive on the narrative of unchecked rival capabilities.

The Sentinel program offers the American public an opportunity to rethink our approach to nuclear strategy. It’s time to ask whether programs like Sentinel are truly necessary or whether they serve the military-industrial complex’s outsized influence.

Breaking free from the military-industrial complex is about more than saving money—it’s about ensuring that U.S. defense policies reflect the needs of the American people.

Danica Raich is an undergraduate student at American University and an Ohio Township resident.
